Main Description

Principles

  • Measures must be simple, objective, easy to collect, easy to interpret, and hard to misinterpret.
  • Measurement collections must be automated and non-intrusive, that is, not interfere with the activities of the developers.
  • Measures must contribute to quality assessment early in the lifecycle, when efforts to improve software quality are effective.
  • Measures absolute values and trends must be actively used by management personnel and engineering personnel for communicating progress and quality in a consistent format.
  • The selection of a basic or more extensive set of measures will depend on the project's characteristics and context: if it is large or has stringent safety or reliability requirements and the development and assessment teams are knowledgeable about measurement, then it may be useful to collect and analyze the technical measures. The contract may require certain measures to be collected, or the organization may be trying to improve it skills an processes in particular areas. There is no simple answer to fit all circumstances, the Project Manager must select what is appropriate when the Measurement Plan is produced. When introducing a measurement program for the first time though, it is sensible to err on the side of simplicity.

A Taxonomy of Measurement

Experience has shown that most information needs can be grouped into general areas, called information categories. Information categories represent key areas of concern. PSM identifies seven information categories:

  • Schedule and progress - Addresses the achievement of project milestones and the completion of individual work units. A project that falls behind schedule can usually meet its delivery objectives only by eliminating functionality or sacrificing product quality.
  • Resources and cost - Relates to the balance between the work to be performed and personnel resources assigned to the project. A project that exceeds the budgeted effort usually can recover only by reducing software functionality or by sacrificing product quality.
  • Product size and stability - Addresses the stability of the functionality or capability required of the software. It also relates to the volume of software delivered to provide the required capability. Stability includes changes in functional scope or quantity. An increase in software size usually requires increasing the applied resources or extending the project schedule.
  • Product quality - Addresses the ability of the delivered software product to support the user's needs without failure. If a poor-quality product is delivered, the burden of making it work usually falls on the assigned maintenance organization.
  • Process performance - Relates to the capability of the supplier relative to the project needs. A supplier with a poor software development process or low productivity may have difficulty meeting aggressive project schedules and cost objectives.
  • Technology effectiveness - Addresses the viability of the proposed technical approach. It addresses engineering approaches such as software reuse, use of commercial software components, reliance on advanced software development processes, and implementation of common software architectures. Cost increases and schedule delays may result if key elements of the proposed technical approach are not achieved.
  • Customer satisfaction - Addresses the degree to which products and services delivered by the project meet the customer's expectations. Indications of satisfaction may be obtained from customer feedback and the levels of customer support required.

The PSM information categories help in selecting appropriate measures. This is accomplished by allocating each identified project information need to one or more of the seven information categories.

Ideas about the entities that should be measured in order to satisfy an information need can also be formulated and organized. These ideas are called measurable concepts.

Please note that it is not necessary to include all measurements to receive benefit. In fact, it is possible (and undesireable in most cases) to measure too much and have diminishing returns. We recommend that when you first establish a measurement program, you start with a small set of measures and build it up based on information needs.

The measurement process and measures also apply to small projects. The difference is in the scale of effort that is spent on measurement activities. Small projects typically have fewer information needs, and therefore, fewer measures. For those measures collected, less data is typically available. For example, while small projects often have information needs related to meeting schedules, there are usually only a few dozen activities tracked with a schedule, versus the hundreds of activities that may exist for a large project.

A Basic Set of Measures

Even the smallest projects will want to track progress to determine if the project is on schedule and on budget, and if not, to re-estimate and determine if scope changes are needed. This minimal measurement set will therefore focus on progress measures.

Schedule and Progress

  • Milestone Completion
    • Milestone Dates - This measure is used to evaluate the schedule, progress, and dependencies of key development activities and dates. This measure is used by project managers to objectively assess the progress of development against the project plan. This information need is addressed by measuring the total days late for all milestones.
  • Work Unit Progress
    • Problem Reports Opened, Closed - This measure is used to track the number of opened and closed problem report. A problem report may or may not be a defect. They are typically analyzed by a change control board to determine whether the problem is a defect.  Also, consider establishing a criteria or threshold to the measure.
    • Test Cases Attempted, Passed - The ultimate measure of completeness is how much functionality has been integrated and tested. This measure is used to determine how much functionality is actually complete. Thus, a very simple way to communicate progress is with a test case progress trend.



    • Test Considerations

      Some test cases may represent significantly more value or effort than others. Don't read too much into this graph - it just provides some assurance that there is progress towards completed functionality.

Resources and Cost

  • Personnel Effort
    • Development Effort - Effort is how much time (units can be hours or days) spent completing a task (analysis, design, code, test, etc.) Effort is monitored to determine the scope of work required. It is typically used for planning similar future work.
  • Financial Performance
    • Cost - Effort can directly be translated into cost. What will cost to complete an engineering task. What will be the estimated cost for the next release? Are we currently within our allocated budget? These are information needs that can be answered by measuring cost.
    • Earned Value - One of the most commonly used method ([ PMI96]) to measure progress is Earned Value Analysis. Projects and organization typically track some level of development effort and cost. To get a deeper understanding of financial performance and to determine whether spending is according to the plan and projected program costs, managers use Earned Value Analysis. The simplest way to measure earned value is to take the sum of the original estimated effort for all completed tasks. A "percent complete" for the project can be computed as the earned value divided by the total original estimated effort for the project. Productivity (or Performance Index) is the earned value divided by the actual effort spend on the complete tasks. For example, suppose the coding effort has been divided into several tasks, many of which are now complete. The original estimate for the completed tasks was 30 effort days. The total estimated effort for the project was 100 days, so we can project that the project is roughly 30% complete.

    • Earned Value Considerations

      • The Performance Index must only be used to adjust estimates for similar tasks. Early completion of requirements gathering tasks does not suggest that coding will complete more quickly. So, compute the Performance Index only for similar kinds of tasks, and adjust estimates only for similar tasks.
      • Consider other factors. Will future tasks be performed by similarly skilled staff under similar conditions? Has the data been contaminated by "outliers" - tasks which were severely over-estimated or under-estimated? Is time being reported consistently (for example, overtime should be included even if not paid)?
      • Are estimates for newer tasks already accounting for the Performance Index? If so, then estimates for new tasks will tend to be closer to the target, pushing the performance index closer to 100%. You should either consistently re-estimate all incomplete tasks, or adopt the following practice from Extreme Programming (XP)[ JEF01] - refer to the original estimates as "points", and measure new tasks in terms of these same "points" without adjusting for actual performance . The advantage of "points" is that increases (or decreases) in performance can be tracked ("project velocity" in XP terminology).

      If tasks are large (more than 5 days), or there are a lot of tasks which are partially complete, you may wish to factor them into your analysis. Apply a subjective "percent completion", multiply this by the task's effort estimate, and include this in the earned value. Greater consistency in results is obtained if there are clear rules for assigning the percent complete. For example, one rule could be that a coding task is assigned no more than 80% complete until the code has passed a code review.

Product Size and Stability

Typically, organizations (or projects) choose a method of measuring product or software size. The two most most popular measures for software size are:

  • Physical Size and Stability
    • Lines of Code - Esimated total size of the code - also know as source lines of code or SLOC. This may change significantly as requirements are better understood and as design solutions mature. Include reused software which is subject to change by the team. NOTE: The definition of a "line of code" or a "statement" varies widely in the industry. But the key is to be consistent on the definition within your organization. All projects within your organization should adopt the same definition of a "line of code".
    • Function Points - The function point metric was devised in 1977 by A. J. Albrecht, then of IBM, as a means of measuring software size and productivity. It uses functional, logical entities such as inputs, outputs, and inquiries as inputs into measures for software size and productivity as appose to lines of code. For more information about function points, vist the International Function Point Users' Group (IFPUG) at mailto:blorenz@us.ibm.com.

    Additionally, sizing by requirements as well as use cases are becoming more popular.

Product Quality

  • Functional Correctness
    • Defects - It is often useful to track the trend of open and closed defects. This provides a rough indication as to whether there is a significant backlog of defect fixing work to be completed and how quickly they are being closed.

  • Defect Considerations
    • All change requests should not have equal weight, whether they affect one line of code or cause major re-design. This can be addressed by some of the following techniques:
      • Be aware of outliers. Change Requests which require substantial work should be identified as such and be tracked as separate tasks, not bundled into a bucket of general bug fixing. If lots of tiny fixes are dominating the trend, then consider grouping them so that each Change Request represents a more consistent unit of work.
      • You can record more information, such as a subjective "effort category" of "less than 1 day" "1 day" "less than 5 days" "more than 5 days".
      • You can record estimated SLOCs and actual SLOCs for each Defect.
    • A lack of defects being recorded may imply a lack of testing. Be aware of the level of test effort occurring when examining defect trends.

A Medium Set of Measures

In addition to the measures identified in the basic set of measures, additional measures can be tracked. These most common additional measures are included in this medium set.

Schedule and Progress

  • Work Unit Progress
    • Requirements Tested - This measure is used to evaluate the status of requirements. Requirements progress can be shown by tracking them through various states (for example, defined, approved, implemented, tested). Ultimately, requirements are fully satisfied when they are tested.
    • Use Case Developed - This measure is used to evaluate the status of use case development. Use cases can be shown by tracking them through various states such as defined, approved, developed.
    • Design Model Developed - This measure is used to evaluate the status of the design model. Design model development can be shown by tracking design elements (such as packages, classes, diagrams, etc).
    • Scenarios Developed - This measure is used to evaluate the status of the scenario development. Scenarios can be shown by tracking when they are defined as well as the completeness of the scenarios.

Resources and Cost

  • Personnel Effort
    • Experience Level - Level of experience can directly affect both schedules (delivering early, on-time, or late) and quality. Therefore, this measure can be important to track. In particular, this measure is often tracked at the organization and the executive levels.
  • Environmental and Support Resources
    • Quantity Needed, Available -

Product Size and Stability

  • Functional Size and Stability
    • Requirements - Managing requirements is a key best practice for software and systems development. Monitoring and controlling requirements are good practices for effective project management. Requirements that are not agreed upon by all stakeholders (unstable) can result in delivering the wrong system and/or a delay in the schedule.

Customer Satisfaction

  • Customer Feedback
    • Satisfaction Ratings - Every project has customer or end-users regardless of whether it's an internal project, a large system, or a product that will be developed and sold. Constant feedback and monitoring of the feedback results is a better understanding of the user needs as well as improved customer relations.


"Add-on" Measures

Add-on measures are additional measures that are usually important in either a certain phase of a project or if there is an underlying need for additional information in an area. For example, if a command and control system is being built that requires maximum performance, reliability, and response time, then tests for system utilization, throughput, and mean-time-to-failure are important measures that should be "added" and tracked.

Schedule and Progress

  • Work Unit Progress
    • Change Requests Opened, Resolved - This measure is used to evaluate the status of change requests for enhancements. Oftentime, this measure is tracked after a product is released and requests for enhancements start being submitted.
  • Incremental Capability
    • Components Integrated - This measure is a product of incremental development. It is useful to assess if the incremental builds are being completed as scheduled.
    • Functionality Integrated - This measure is also a product of incremental development.

Resources and Cost

  • Personnel Effort
    • Staff Turnover - This is an important measure to track if the project is experience increasing staff turnover. Increasing turnover can result in schedule slip and higher costs as new members must go through a training period before becoming productive.

Product Size and Stability

  • Physical Size and Stability
    • Database Size - This measure is of course important if the product is a database project or contains a database component.
  • Functional Size and Stability
    • Components, Classes, Objects - A project practicing object-oriented design can measure size by monitoring design components, classics, packages, and objects.

Product Quality

  • Functional Correctness
    • Age of Defects - This measure becomes more important when a product or project is mature or has been released. If this indicator shows that age of defects increases, this may indicate a problem in quality - defects are not be resolved - and the lack of resources to fix defects
    • Defect Density - This measure can be used to evaluate code quality by monitoring the number of defects per thousand lines of code.
  • Maintainability
    • Time to Restore, Cyclomatic Complexity - Overly complex code is hard to maintain. This affects quality.
  • Efficiency
    • Utilization, Throughput, Response Time - Projects that have system requirements for efficiency may wish to track measurements for system utilization, data/messaging throughput, and user interface response time.
  • Portability
    • Standards Compliance - A project that requires highly portable code may have strict requirements for coding, interface, data, and other standards.
  • Modularity
    • Classes/Categories Modified Per Change - If modularity is a problem area, you may wish to track this measure.
  • Usability
    • Operator Errors - For an interactive system, you may wish to track the number of operator errors if that is an important information need.
  • Reliability
    • Mean-Time-to-Failure - On a project that requires high reliability, such as a command and control system, or perhaps an air traffic control system, reliability is an important information need and mean-time-to-failure is an effective measure that can be tracked.
  • Adaptability
    • Average Person-hours Per Change - A system that is adaptable may require less-experienced developers. Tracking the average amount of effort required to make a change can provide the management data for planning personnel during a product maintenance phase.

Process Performance

  • Process Compliance
    • Reference Maturity Rating - For an organization attempting to achieve a maturity assessment rating at a certain SEI CMM/CMMI level, this measure can satisfy the information need.
    • Process Audit Findings - Again, for an organization attempting to achieve a maturity assessment the results of process audits can be measured and tracked to show increasing process compliance
  • Process Efficiency
    • Requirements Traced -
    • Productivity - The efficiency of the software development process can be evaluated by comparing achieved productivity to bid rates. Productivity provides an indication of the amount of work produced relative to the effort expended. Lower than expected productivity may lead to effort and cost overruns.
    • Cycle Time -
  • Process Effectiveness
    • Defects Contained - Tracking when defects are fixed. Which phase (or when) defects are detected and which phase they are fixed can provide insight into the quality of the development process.
    • Defects Escaping - Tracking defects that are escaping a phase and "living" into a new phase.
    • Rework Effort, Components - Rework corresponds to the an increase in quality. It also can show the effectiveness of the process. For example, certain amounts of rework is necessary in any software development process. Early rework is a good sign of progress since rework early is less expensive that rework late. Continuous rework, late rework, or even zero rework due to the absence of a configured baseline are indicators of negative quality and possibly a problem in the process.

Technology Effectiveness

Projects that rely heavily on new technology, off-the-shelf products, or advanced technology may want to consider the following measures.

  • Technology Suitability
    • Requirements Coverage - This measure can be used to evaluate the ability of a software package to satisfy customer expectations and requirements.
  • Technology Volatility
    • Baseline Changes - If the software package results in baseline changes (both expected and unexpected), then cost increases and schedule delays may result.


Customer Satisfaction

  • Customer Feedback
    • Satisfaction Ratings - Every project has customer or end-users regardless of whether it's an internal project, a large system, or a product that will be developed and sold. Constant feedback and monitoring of the feedback results is a better understanding of the user needs as well as improved customer relations.
    • Award Fee - Award fees based on cost, schedule and milestone achievements that exceed expected results can be used to gauge customer levels of satisfaction.
  • Customer Support
    • Requests for Support - Increasing requests for support can be a sign of a complex product or system which can result in an increasingly dissatisfied customer.

Additional Measures for a Complete Measurement Set

In addition to the measures described in the previous sections, the following measures can be added to form a "complete" measurement set.

Schedule and Progress

  • Critical Path Convergence
    • Slack Time -
  • Work Unit Progress
    • Requirements Traced - Progress can be shown by tracking how or when requirements are traced to other work units. For example requirements can be traced to design elements as well as test cases.
    • Reviews Completed - Some project schedule design and code reviews during the software development lifecycle. Completing the reviews show progress.
    • Action Items Opened, Completed - Action items are opened and assigned. These can be tracked to show they are completed in a timely manner.
